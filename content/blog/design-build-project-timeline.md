Ask ten owners why they chose design-build and most will say "speed." They are right, but the reason is usually misunderstood. Design-build is not faster because a single firm rushes the drawings. It is faster because it removes the seams between phases — the gaps where a traditional project quietly loses weeks it can never recover.

## The problem with the traditional handoff

In the conventional design-bid-build model, three things happen in sequence. First, an architect and engineers produce a complete set of documents. Then those documents go out to bid, and a general contractor is selected weeks or months later. Only then does construction begin. Each transition is a full stop. Nobody pricing the building was in the room when it was drawn, so the first real cost feedback arrives after the design is already finished — often after it is too expensive.

When the numbers come back high, the project goes backward. The design gets reopened, value-engineered, and re-priced. That loop can run several times before a shovel touches the ground. The calendar damage is rarely on the critical path anyone planned for; it hides in redesign cycles and re-bidding.

## Where design-build recovers the time

Under a single design-build contract, the builder and the design team work for the same entity from day one. That changes the sequence in three concrete ways.

**Design and construction overlap.** You do not have to finish 100 percent of the drawings before work begins. Site clearing, grading, and foundations can be released for construction while the building's interior details are still being finalized. This phased release — often called fast-tracking — is only responsible because the people building the foundation and the people drawing the walls answer to the same team.

**Pricing happens continuously, not at the end.** Because estimators see the design as it develops, cost feedback is immediate. A structural decision that would blow the budget gets flagged in the week it is made, not in a bid opening two months later. There is no redesign loop because the design never gets far enough off-budget to require one.

**Procurement starts early.** Long-lead items — structural steel, electrical gear, rooftop units, elevators — are the quiet schedule killers on commercial projects. In a single-contract structure, the builder can identify and release those orders while design continues, so the material is arriving as the site becomes ready for it rather than months after.

## What the compression actually looks like

The savings are real but they are not magic. On a typical ground-up commercial building, the overlap between design and construction and the elimination of a separate bid phase commonly removes a meaningful block of calendar time versus running the same scope in strict sequence. The exact figure depends on the building type, the site, and how much of the design can be responsibly released early — which is precisely why we will not quote you a universal number. Any contractor who promises a fixed percentage before seeing your site is selling, not estimating.

What matters more than the headline number is *certainty*. A compressed schedule that slips is worth less than a slightly longer one that holds. Design-build tends to hold because the same team owns both the drawings and the means to build them; there is no one to point at when something is missed, so fewer things are missed.

## What this asks of you as the owner

Design-build's schedule advantage depends on early, committed decisions. Because construction starts before every detail is drawn, changing your mind about the building's core program after foundations are in costs real time and money. The model rewards owners who do their thinking up front and then hold the line. If your project needs to keep evolving well into construction, that is a legitimate need — but it is worth naming early, because it shapes how aggressively the schedule can be compressed.

## The bottom line

Design-build does not save time by working faster. It saves time by removing the handoffs, the redesign loops, and the standalone bidding phase that a traditional project treats as normal. For self-storage, hospitality, and multifamily owners measuring the cost of every month until a building is generating revenue, that recovered time is usually the whole reason to choose the model — and the reason experienced developers keep coming back to it.
