For a franchised hotel, a property improvement plan is not optional. It is the brand's list of required upgrades — finishes, systems, guest-facing amenities — that a property must complete to keep its flag or to earn one. The document arrives with a scope and a deadline, and the owner faces a construction project with two constraints most projects never have to reconcile: the work has to satisfy a brand standard, and it usually has to happen while guests are checking in down the hall.

## What a PIP is and where it comes from

A PIP originates with the franchisor. It is triggered by a change of ownership, a franchise renewal, a brand-wide standard update, or a property falling behind on quality scores. The brand inspects the property, compares it against current standards for that flag, and issues a plan detailing what must be brought up to spec and by when. For a select-service property carrying a flag like Microtel by Wyndham, the plan protects the consistency that guests expect from the brand nationwide.

The scope ranges widely. A light PIP might be soft goods — carpet, case goods, bedding, paint, and lighting in the guest rooms. A heavy PIP can reach into the building's bones: reconfigured lobbies and breakfast areas, exterior and signage updates, and mechanical, electrical, or life-safety systems that have aged past standard. The owner's first task is to read the plan against reality and understand which category theirs falls into, because that determines everything about cost, schedule, and disruption.

## The defining constraint: an occupied building

Most PIPs are executed while the hotel keeps operating. Closing the property means zero revenue against a running debt service and payroll, so owners overwhelmingly choose to renovate around guests. That single fact reshapes the entire project. The construction has to coexist with a functioning hospitality business, and the two have very different tolerances for noise, dust, smell, and disruption.

This is not a normal jobsite. Guests who paid for a room expect quiet at night and a clean, safe path to the elevator by day. A renovation that treats the building as empty will generate complaints, refunds, and brand-score damage that can outlast the construction itself. Managing that coexistence is the real discipline of a PIP, and it is why hospitality renovation is its own specialty rather than general contracting applied to a hotel.

## How phased construction keeps the doors open

The mechanism that makes an occupied renovation work is phasing. Instead of touching the whole building at once, the builder isolates a portion — often a floor, a wing, or a stack of rooms — takes it out of the rental inventory, completes it fully, returns it to service, and moves to the next. The hotel sells the rooms that are not under construction while the crews work the ones that are.

Phasing is planned with the operator, not imposed on them. The sequence has to respect occupancy patterns, group bookings, and seasonal demand, so the rooms out of service at any moment are the ones the property can most afford to lose. Done well, phasing lets a property complete a full-building renovation while never dropping below a workable inventory — the difference between a PIP that funds itself with continuing revenue and one that bleeds cash.

## Managing the guest experience through the work

Beyond phasing, an occupied renovation lives or dies on the details of coexistence. Several practices separate a smooth PIP from a disruptive one:

- **Noise discipline.** The loudest work — demolition, core drilling, anything that carries through the structure — is scheduled for daytime hours when occupancy is lowest, never overnight.
- **Dust and air separation.** Active work zones are sealed off from guest areas, with attention to shared HVAC so construction dust and odors do not migrate into sold rooms.
- **Clean, separated circulation.** Construction traffic, deliveries, and debris removal use routes that keep them out of the guest experience, ideally on a schedule and path guests never encounter.
- **Buffer zones.** Where possible, unsold rooms are left between the active work and occupied rooms, so no paying guest sleeps on the other side of a construction wall.

These are not luxuries. They are the operational fabric that lets a hotel keep its scores and its guests while a building project runs through it.

## Schedule pressure and the brand deadline

A PIP carries a hard completion deadline set by the franchisor, and missing it can put the flag itself at risk. That raises the stakes on the schedule beyond an ordinary renovation, where a slip costs time and money but not the brand affiliation the property's value depends on. The phased approach that protects revenue also stretches the calendar compared to gutting an empty building, so the schedule has to be planned realistically from the start.

The long-lead items are the usual suspects — FF&E packages, custom millwork, and any mechanical equipment — and the same procurement discipline that serves ground-up work applies here. Ordering the brand-specified finishes and fixtures early, before a phase needs them, is how a builder keeps the sequence moving toward a deadline that will not move for them.

## The bottom line

A hotel PIP is a renovation defined by two things a normal project is not: a brand standard that dictates the scope and a full house that constrains the method. Phased construction is what reconciles them, letting an owner meet the franchisor's requirements and deadline while the property keeps selling rooms. The work is as much operations as it is construction, and the builders who do it well are the ones who treat the guest experience as part of the job — not an obstacle to it.
