Self-storage has a reputation as the simplest thing you can build — four walls, a roof, and a lot of doors. That reputation is why it trips up so many first-time developers. The building is straightforward; the *project* is not. The path from raw land to a certificate of occupancy runs through entitlements, sitework, and a construction sequence where the quiet risks are rarely the ones a newcomer expects. Here is the whole route, and where projects tend to stall.

## Phase one: site selection and feasibility

Everything starts with the land, and not every parcel that pencils on a spreadsheet will actually support a storage facility. Feasibility is the discipline of finding out before you are committed. Zoning is the first gate: many municipalities restrict self-storage or treat it as a conditional use requiring special approval. A parcel zoned wrong is not a hurdle — it can be a dead end, and it is far cheaper to learn that in week one than after closing.

Beyond zoning, feasibility means understanding the site's physical reality. Topography, soil conditions, wetlands, floodplain, and utility availability all shape what can be built and what it will cost. A flat, dry, well-served site and a sloped, wet, unserved one can carry the same asking price and radically different development costs. The role of early due diligence is to price that difference before it becomes your problem.

## Phase two: entitlements and permitting

Once a site is under control, the entitlement phase begins — securing the governmental approvals to build. This is where storage schedules most often slip, because it is largely outside the developer's direct control. Site plan review, stormwater management approval, and sometimes a special-use permit or variance each move at the pace of a municipal calendar, not yours.

The most common entitlement surprise is stormwater. Storage facilities cover a large share of their site with impervious roof and pavement, which municipalities scrutinize closely for runoff. The stormwater management system — detention, retention, or infiltration — can consume a meaningful slice of the site and the budget, and designing it is not a formality. Engaging civil engineering early, and a builder who has navigated the local approval process before, is the single best protection against an entitlement phase that drags for quarters instead of months.

## Phase three: sitework — the underestimated cost center

Once permits are in hand, construction begins with sitework, and this is where inexperienced pro formas break. On many greenfield storage sites, the work below and around the building — clearing, mass grading, the stormwater system, utilities, paving, and drive aisles — represents a larger share of total cost than the building itself. That is a reversal from the intuition that the structure is the expensive part.

Sitework is also where the ground delivers its surprises. Poor soils may require undercutting and replacement or engineered fill; rock can appear where borings suggested it would not. Thorough geotechnical investigation during preconstruction is how a serious builder shrinks this risk, but on a raw site it can never be reduced to zero. A budget with a realistic sitework line and an honest contingency is a sign the project was estimated by someone who has actually built one.

## Phase four: building construction

With the pad ready, the building itself goes up quickly — which is exactly why storage is attractive to build. Most modern facilities use a pre-engineered metal building system or a light structural steel frame, both of which erect fast and predictably once the site and foundations are in. For climate-controlled facilities, the added scope is mechanical: insulation, HVAC, and the controls to hold temperature and humidity across a large, compartmentalized interior.

The critical-path risk in this phase is procurement, not labor. Structural steel, the building system package, HVAC equipment, and roll-up doors all carry lead times that can stretch for months. On a well-run project, those long-lead orders are released during preconstruction so material arrives as the site becomes ready — one of the clearest advantages of a design-build structure, where the builder can commit to procurement while design and sitework proceed in parallel.

## Phase five: closeout and certificate of occupancy

The finish line is the certificate of occupancy — the municipality's sign-off that the building is safe and complete for its intended use. Reaching it means passing final inspections across building, fire, electrical, and site disciplines, and closing out the punch list of small corrections that every project generates.

The CO is frequently gated by items that have nothing to do with the building envelope: final site paving and striping, landscaping required by the approved site plan, fire lane access, and functioning life-safety systems. Experienced builders sequence these so they are not the thing standing between a finished building and revenue. A storage facility that is structurally done but cannot open because the landscaping specified in the site plan was treated as an afterthought is a self-inflicted delay — and an avoidable one.

## The bottom line

Ground-up self-storage is simple to build and complex to develop. The value is created and protected in the phases before the building goes up — feasibility, entitlements, and sitework — and in a procurement strategy that keeps long-lead material off the critical path. Owners who respect that sequence, and partner with a builder who has run it before, get to a certificate of occupancy on schedule. Those who assume the simplicity of the building extends to the project learn otherwise, usually in the sitework line and the stormwater permit.
